Architecture Models

Architecture questions test tradeoffs. Understand where responsibility sits, how components communicate, what is shared, and how design choices affect resilience, cost, scalability, responsiveness, and security.

Core lesson

  • Cloud shared responsibility across IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS
  • Serverless, microservices, APIs, and infrastructure as code
  • Logical segmentation, software-defined networking, and cloud networking
  • Virtualization, containers, IoT, embedded systems, ICS/SCADA, and edge computing
  • Centralized versus decentralized designs
  • On-premises, cloud, hybrid, multi-cloud, and managed services
  • Architecture considerations: availability, resilience, cost, responsiveness, scalability, ease of deployment, and risk transference

Exam focus

  • In IaaS the customer generally manages guest OSs and applications; in SaaS the provider manages the application platform.
  • Containers share a host kernel and are not identical to full VMs.
  • Architecture choices are rarely “most secure” in isolation; use the stated business constraint.

Applying Security Principles

This section is about placing and configuring network controls. Identify the trust boundary, desired traffic flow, inspection point, failure behavior, and administrative path before selecting a firewall, proxy, IPS, jump server, VPN, or access-control mechanism.

Core lesson

  • Security zones, screened subnets, management networks, and attack-surface reduction
  • Inline versus passive sensors and fail-open versus fail-closed behavior
  • Jump servers, proxies, load balancers, sensors, collectors, and network taps
  • Port security, IEEE 802.1X, EAP, NAC, and device posture
  • Packet-filtering, stateful, proxy, UTM, NGFW, and web application firewalls
  • VPNs, IPsec, TLS, SD-WAN, SASE, and secure communication paths

Exam focus

  • An IDS alerts; an IPS is inline and can block.
  • A WAF focuses on HTTP application traffic; a stateful firewall tracks connection state.
  • A jump server centralizes administrative access; it does not replace least privilege.
  • Fail-open preserves availability; fail-closed prioritizes security.

Objective 3.3 · 20 practice questions

Protecting Data

Protect data according to its type, classification, state, ownership, geography, and business value. The exam often provides a data-handling requirement and asks which control best satisfies it.

Core lesson

  • Data types: regulated, trade secret, intellectual property, legal, financial, human- and machine-readable
  • Classification labels and handling requirements
  • Data at rest, in transit, and in use
  • Data sovereignty, residency, and geographic restrictions
  • Encryption, hashing, tokenization, masking, obfuscation, and segmentation
  • Data loss prevention and access restrictions
  • Ownership, custodianship, retention, minimization, and lifecycle handling

Exam focus

  • Encryption protects confidentiality; hashing verifies integrity.
  • Tokenization replaces sensitive values with references; masking hides selected portions.
  • Data in use is actively processed and may require memory or enclave protections.
  • Classification drives handling; it is not itself the technical protection.

Objective 3.4 · 20 practice questions

Resiliency and Recovery

Resiliency keeps services operating through failure; recovery restores them after disruption. Choose solutions based on required availability, acceptable data loss, restoration time, cost, and test evidence.

Core lesson

  • High availability, clustering, load balancing, replication, redundancy, and geographic dispersion
  • Hot, warm, and cold sites; active-active and active-passive designs
  • Capacity planning for people, technology, facilities, and demand
  • Tabletop, simulation, parallel, and full-interruption recovery tests
  • Full, incremental, differential, snapshot, replication, and offline/immutable backups
  • Backup frequency, encryption, retention, and restore validation
  • UPS technologies, generators, power distribution, and environmental resilience

Exam focus

  • RAID improves availability but is not a backup.
  • RPO is acceptable data loss measured in time; RTO is acceptable restoration time.
  • A backup is unproven until a restore test succeeds.
  • A hot site is fastest and most expensive; a cold site requires the most setup.
