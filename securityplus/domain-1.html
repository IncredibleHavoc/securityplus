Security Controls

Security controls are classified in two dimensions: what kind of control they are and what job they perform. On the exam, identify both dimensions from the scenario instead of memorizing a single label for a product.

Core lesson

  • Control categories: technical, managerial, operational, and physical
  • Control functions: preventive, deterrent, detective, corrective, compensating, and directive
  • A control can fit one category and one function at the same time
  • Compensating controls reduce risk when the preferred control is unavailable
  • The primary purpose in the scenario determines the best classification

Exam focus

  • A firewall rule is normally technical and preventive; a firewall alert can be technical and detective.
  • A guard is operational because a person performs the control; a lock is physical.
  • Restoring from backup is corrective even though the backup was created before the incident.

Security Concepts

This section establishes the security outcomes and trust decisions behind most scenario questions. Learn to translate a business requirement into confidentiality, integrity, availability, authentication, authorization, accounting, zero trust, physical protection, or deception.

Core lesson

  • CIA triad: confidentiality, integrity, and availability
  • Non-repudiation and the role of hashes, signatures, and protected private keys
  • AAA: authentication proves identity, authorization grants permissions, accounting records activity
  • Gap analysis compares current and desired security states
  • Zero trust separates policy decisions from enforcement and continuously evaluates context
  • Physical security: barriers, access control vestibules, cameras, sensors, guards, and environmental controls
  • Deception and disruption: honeypots, honeynets, honeytokens, and decoy data

Exam focus

  • Authentication answers “Who are you?”; authorization answers “What may you do?”
  • A hash alone supports integrity, not confidentiality or signer identity.
  • Zero trust does not mean “trust nobody”; it means no implicit trust based on network location.
  • Choose the control that most directly satisfies the stated security objective.

Objective 1.3 · 12 practice questions

Change Management

Change management reduces outages, security drift, and unauthorized modifications. The exam often asks what must happen before, during, or after a change, especially when risk, dependencies, approval, testing, rollback, and documentation compete as answer choices.

Core lesson

  • Request, ownership, scope, impact analysis, approval, testing, implementation, validation, and closure
  • Standard, normal, and emergency changes
  • Maintenance windows and stakeholder communication
  • Backout or rollback plans and restoration criteria
  • Technical dependencies, version control, configuration baselines, and documentation updates
  • Separation of development, approval, and production deployment where practical

Exam focus

  • Emergency does not mean undocumented; approval and records are expedited, not abandoned.
  • Test before broad deployment and validate after implementation.
  • A rollback plan answers how to return to the last known-good state.
  • Unauthorized changes should be stopped or reversed according to procedure.

Objective 1.4 · 18 practice questions

Cryptographic Solutions

Cryptography questions are usually about selecting the right property or key operation. Separate confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and non-repudiation, then identify whether encryption, hashing, signatures, certificates, or obfuscation provides it.

Core lesson

  • Symmetric encryption for efficient bulk confidentiality; asymmetric encryption for key exchange, signatures, and identity
  • Public and private key relationships
  • Key exchange and session-key establishment
  • PKI roles, certificate chains, CSRs, revocation, CRLs, OCSP, and stapling
  • HSMs, TPMs, secure enclaves, and protected key storage
  • Hashing, salting, key stretching, collision resistance, and digital signatures
  • Tokenization, masking, steganography, and obfuscation
  • Blockchain integrity through linked hashes and distributed validation

Exam focus

  • Encrypt with the recipient’s public key for confidentiality; sign with the sender’s private key for authenticity and non-repudiation.
  • Digital signatures do not hide the message.
  • Base64 is encoding, not encryption.
  • A salt prevents identical passwords from producing identical hashes and weakens precomputed attacks.
