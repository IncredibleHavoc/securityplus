Objective 5.1 · 20 practice questions

Security Governance

Governance defines authority, direction, responsibility, and accountability. Distinguish policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines, then connect them to legal, regulatory, contractual, and business requirements.

Core lesson

  • Policies establish management intent and mandatory direction
  • Standards define specific mandatory requirements
  • Procedures define repeatable steps; guidelines provide recommendations
  • Acceptable use, information security, business continuity, disaster recovery, incident response, and data policies
  • Legal, regulatory, contractual, industry, geographic, and business considerations
  • Data owner, controller, processor, custodian/steward, and user responsibilities
  • Governance boards, committees, reporting, exceptions, and review cycles

Exam focus

  • Policy is high-level; procedure is step-by-step.
  • A standard is mandatory and specific; a guideline is recommended.
  • The data owner typically determines classification and access requirements; custodians implement handling controls.

Objective 5.2 · 20 practice questions

Risk Management

Risk management identifies, analyzes, treats, monitors, and communicates uncertainty. Combine likelihood, impact, exposure, and business context instead of treating a vulnerability score as the entire risk decision.

Core lesson

  • Qualitative and quantitative risk assessments
  • Threat, vulnerability, likelihood, impact, inherent risk, residual risk, and control effectiveness
  • Risk appetite, tolerance, threshold, owner, and register
  • Ad hoc, recurring, continuous, and event-driven assessments
  • Risk responses: mitigate, transfer, accept, avoid, and sometimes share
  • SLE, ARO, ALE, asset value, and exposure factor
  • Business impact analysis, dependencies, maximum tolerable downtime, RTO, RPO, MTTR, and MTBF

Exam focus

  • SLE = asset value × exposure factor; ALE = SLE × annual rate of occurrence.
  • Risk acceptance requires an authorized owner and documentation.
  • Insurance transfers financial impact but does not remove operational risk.
  • BIA identifies business impact and recovery priorities; it is not a vulnerability scan.

Objective 5.3 · 16 practice questions

Third-party Risk

Third parties extend the organization’s attack surface and dependency chain. Assess them before onboarding, define enforceable requirements, monitor performance, and plan for termination or service failure.

Core lesson

  • Vendor due diligence, questionnaires, evidence, references, and breach history
  • Supply-chain analysis and concentration risk
  • Right-to-audit, notification, data handling, security, privacy, retention, and deletion clauses
  • Ongoing monitoring, reassessment, performance metrics, and exception tracking
  • SLA, MOU, MOA, NDA, BPA, master service agreement, and work order/statement of work
  • Onboarding, access control, offboarding, and data return/destruction
  • Vendor lock-in, geographic risk, subcontractors, and dependency management

Exam focus

  • An SLA defines measurable service commitments; an NDA protects confidential information.
  • A right-to-audit clause provides contractual authority to assess controls.
  • SOC reports and certifications are evidence inputs, not guarantees of security.

Objective 5.4 · 14 practice questions

Security Compliance

Compliance means meeting applicable obligations and proving it. Determine which law, regulation, standard, contract, or privacy requirement applies, then identify monitoring, reporting, retention, and consequence requirements.

Core lesson

  • Regulatory, legal, contractual, industry, and internal compliance
  • Privacy principles: purpose limitation, minimization, consent, transparency, access, correction, retention, and deletion
  • Data inventory, processing records, data location, and cross-border considerations
  • Monitoring, reporting, evidence, attestations, and remediation
  • Consequences: fines, sanctions, contract loss, litigation, reputational harm, and operational restrictions
  • Data subject, controller, processor, owner, and custodian roles
  • Common contexts such as payment-card, healthcare, financial, educational, and government data

Exam focus

  • Compliance does not equal security; it defines minimum obligations for a particular scope.
  • Data minimization limits collection to what is necessary.
  • Retention requirements may require keeping data, while privacy rules may require deleting it when no longer justified—apply the governing requirement.

Objective 5.5 · 14 practice questions

Audits and Assessments

Audits evaluate whether controls meet defined criteria; assessments measure posture or effectiveness. Understand who performs the work, what evidence is gathered, the scope, and how findings are reported and remediated.

Core lesson

  • Internal, external, independent, regulatory, and self-assessments
  • Control design versus operating effectiveness
  • Evidence requests, sampling, interviews, observation, and technical testing
  • Attestations, audit reports, findings, exceptions, management responses, and corrective-action plans
  • Penetration-test perspectives: known, partially known, and unknown environments
  • Reconnaissance, enumeration, exploitation, post-exploitation, cleanup, and reporting
  • Scope, authorization, rules of engagement, and safe testing windows

Exam focus

  • An internal audit is performed by or for the organization; an external audit uses an outside party.
  • A penetration test proves exploitability within scope; it does not certify complete security.
  • Known-environment testing provides more internal knowledge; unknown-environment testing better simulates an external attacker.

Objective 5.6 · 16 practice questions

Security Awareness

Awareness changes recognition and reporting behavior; training builds role-specific capability. Programs should be continuous, measurable, accessible, and adjusted using results and changing threats.

Core lesson

  • Phishing simulations, reporting channels, and feedback
  • Recognizing anomalous behavior, social engineering, removable-media risks, and data handling mistakes
  • Role-based training for users, developers, administrators, executives, and third parties
  • Initial, recurring, event-driven, and just-in-time education
  • Metrics: completion, reporting rate, repeat failures, click rate, time to report, and knowledge retention
  • Positive reinforcement, targeted remediation, and culture
  • Policy acknowledgment and documentation

Exam focus

  • A low click rate is useful, but reporting rate and time to report better measure defensive behavior.
  • Awareness is broad recognition; training teaches skills; education develops deeper understanding.
  • Repeated failure should trigger targeted coaching, not simply broader generic training.
